Is your lot actually ADA compliant?

Most property managers think they are. Many are not. Here is what New York parking lots are required to have, and what it costs to get it wrong.

ADA compliance is one of those things that stays quietly off the radar until it becomes a very loud, very expensive problem. A faded access aisle or a missing sign does not feel urgent until a complaint, an inspection, or a lawsuit puts it front and center. The good news is that a compliant parking lot is entirely achievable, and for most properties it is a straightforward fix. This guide covers what you need to know as a Long Island or NYC property manager.

Why it matters here

Parking lot accessibility is governed by the federal Americans with Disabilities Act, and New York layers its own building and accessibility code on top. For an owner or manager, that means your lot has to meet the stricter of the applicable standards. The exposure is real: accessibility complaints and demand letters are common, and the cost of a claim, plus the rushed remediation that follows, dwarfs the cost of doing it right the first time.

What a compliant lot needs

Every lot is different, but compliant parking almost always comes down to these elements.

Enough accessible spaces

The number of required accessible spaces scales with the total number of spaces in the lot. A small lot needs at least one. Larger lots need more, and a share of them must be van accessible. Getting the count right for your specific lot is the first thing we check.

Properly sized access aisles

Every accessible space needs an adjoining striped access aisle so a person can deploy a ramp or lift and move safely to the building. Van accessible spaces need a wider aisle. These aisles must be marked, and they cannot be used as parking.

Correct signage and symbols

Each accessible space needs the international symbol of accessibility, both painted on the pavement and posted on a sign mounted at the required height. Van accessible spaces need the additional van designation.

An accessible route

The path from the accessible spaces to the entrance has to be usable, which ties into crosswalks, curb ramps, and slope. This is where a lot of lots quietly fail, because the spaces exist but the route to the door was never marked.

The most common mistakes we find

When we walk a lot for a compliance check, the same issues come up again and again:

  • Faded symbols and access aisles that are no longer clearly visible.
  • Too few accessible spaces for the size of the lot.
  • Van accessible spaces missing the wider aisle or the van signage.
  • Signs mounted too low, damaged, or missing entirely.
  • No marked accessible route from the spaces to the entrance.

None of these are hard to fix. The trouble is that most owners do not know they exist until someone points them out. We have walked onto properties where the manager had no idea they were out of compliance until we showed them, and one visit turned a liability into a solved problem.
