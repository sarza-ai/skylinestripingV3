7 signs it is time to restripe.

Faded lines do not just look tired. They cost you customers, capacity, and compliance. Here is how to know your lot is due.

Parking lot lines are easy to ignore right up until they become a problem. Paint fades slowly, so the decline is hard to notice day to day, and by the time a lot looks obviously worn it has usually been costing the business for a while. If you are wondering whether your lot needs attention, these are the seven signs we tell every property manager to watch for.

1. The lines are visibly faded

The obvious one. If you have to look twice to find a stall line, so does every driver who pulls in. Faded paint is the clearest signal that a restripe is overdue, and it is the cheapest problem to fix before it turns into the others on this list.

2. Cars are parking crooked or spreading out

When drivers cannot read the lines they improvise, and a lot built for 200 cars quietly starts holding 160. That lost capacity is real money during peak hours. Sharp, visible stalls pack the lot the way it was designed to be packed.

3. Your ADA markings are worn or wrong

Faded accessibility symbols, unreadable access aisles, and missing van markings are not cosmetic. They are legal exposure. If your compliance markings are fading, treat it as urgent and read our ADA compliance guide to see what your lot needs.

4. You just sealcoated or repaved

Fresh asphalt or a new sealcoat covers the old lines completely. New striping is not optional at that point, it is the final step that makes the surface usable. Timing the restripe right after the surface work gives you the cleanest possible result.

5. Your layout no longer fits your needs

Maybe you need more stalls, a new loading zone, added accessible spaces, or a safer pedestrian path. A restripe is the moment to redesign the lot around how it actually gets used, not just repaint what was there.

6. The lot looks neglected

Your parking lot is the first thing every customer sees, the handshake before they reach the door. A faded, patchy lot tells people you have stopped paying attention. Fresh lines tell them the opposite. For a national brand or a well run property, that impression is worth protecting.

7. It has been more than a year or two

As a rule of thumb, a busy commercial lot benefits from a restripe every one to two years, sooner for high traffic sites and harsh winters, which Long Island delivers reliably. If you cannot remember the last time it was done, it is probably time.
